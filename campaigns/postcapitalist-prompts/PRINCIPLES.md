# Postcapitalist Design Prompts — Campaign Principles

## What This Is

A weekly series of provocative questions shared on LinkedIn (and potentially other platforms) that surface the design tensions at the heart of Mrki — and, more broadly, at the heart of building technology that serves people instead of extracting from them.

Each prompt is an honest question, not a marketing message. The kind of question that keeps you up at night when you're trying to build something that doesn't become what it replaced.

---

## Why This Exists

Mrki's thesis lives in a repository. The research briefs are 20,000 words each. The UX philosophy document names tensions most companies pretend don't exist. None of this is visible to anyone.

This campaign makes the *thinking* visible — not the product, not the pitch, but the questions. The questions are the interesting part. They're what separate "we're building a better social network" (which everyone claims) from "we're genuinely wrestling with problems no one has solved" (which almost no one does in public).

The goals are:

1. **Find the people who care about these questions.** They exist — in design, in education, in youth work, in platform governance, in policy, in philosophy. They're scattered. This is a signal fire.

2. **Start conversations that are actually useful.** Not engagement theater. Not "great post!" comments. Conversations where someone says something we hadn't considered, or challenges an assumption we're holding too tightly, or shares a reference we didn't know about.

3. **Build a body of public thinking** that demonstrates Mrki's intellectual seriousness — not through polished marketing, but through the quality of questions asked and the honesty of the exploration.

## Core Principles

### 1. People Do the Talking

A local agent model helps prepare prompts, maintain cadence, track responses, and propose analysis. But **every word published on social media is written, reviewed, and posted by a human being**. The agent is a research assistant and a calendar, not a ghostwriter and not a community manager.

Why: because the whole point of Mrki is that humans govern, humans connect, humans decide. If we automate our own public voice, we've undermined the thesis before we've shipped the product. Also — people can tell. The uncanny valley of AI-generated "thought leadership" is a trust destroyer, and trust is literally our currency.

### 2. Questions, Not Answers

Every prompt is a genuine question — something we're actually wrestling with. Not a rhetorical question designed to lead people toward our conclusion. Not a "hot take" dressed as a question.

The test: **would we change our design if someone gave us a convincing answer we didn't expect?** If yes, it's a real question. If no, it's marketing.

### 3. Provocative Means Honest, Not Inflammatory

"Provocative" doesn't mean contrarian for clicks. It means saying the thing that's true but uncomfortable. Naming the tension that everyone in the room knows about but no one wants to be the first to say.

Examples of honest provocation:
- "What if invisible reputation is just invisible power — and invisible power is unaccountable power?"
- "Every community in history degraded when it got popular. Why would yours be different?"
- "If teens are governing the platform, what happens when they vote for something you disagree with?"

Examples of dishonest provocation (avoid):
- "Social media is DESTROYING our children" (fear-mongering for engagement)
- "We solved the social media crisis" (we haven't built anything yet)
- "Here's why every other platform got it wrong" (self-serving framing)

### 4. The Metrics Are Not the Metrics

We do not optimize for:
- Likes
- Follower count
- Impressions
- "Virality"
- Reach

These are the metrics of the attention economy we're building against. Using them to measure our success would be ironic at best and corrosive at worst.

### 5. Success Is Measured in Relationships and Artifacts

We measure two things, aligned with the club's two goals — **have fun** and **make stuff**:

**Relationships formed:**
- Did someone reach out because of a prompt? (DM, email, comment that starts a real conversation)
- Did we discover a person, a project, or a reference we didn't know about?
- Did someone offer to collaborate, contribute, or challenge us in a sustained way?
- Did an interesting person engage more than once? (Repeat engagement = genuine interest, not algorithmic coincidence)

**Artifacts produced:**
- Did a conversation lead to a new insight that changed our thinking or design?
- Did a prompt generate a response good enough to feature, cite, or build on?
- Did we produce a body of questions that, taken together, constitute a useful public resource on postcapitalist design?
- Did the series itself become a thing people reference — "have you seen the Mrki design prompts?"

### 6. Cadence Over Volume

One prompt per week. Not two. Not "bonus content." Not "we had a great idea so let's post it now."

Weekly cadence is sustainable. It gives people time to think before responding. It gives us time to read and reflect on responses before posting again. It mirrors the intentional pacing we're designing into Mrki itself.

If we can't maintain the cadence, we skip a week honestly rather than publishing something half-baked to fill a slot.

### 7. Feature Responses, Not Followers

When someone gives an interesting response — a challenge, a new angle, a reference, a disagreement — we feature it. Not as "engagement bait" but as genuine intellectual respect: "this person said something we hadn't considered, and here's how it changed our thinking."

The goal is to make the series feel like a **seminar**, not a broadcast. A place where showing up with a good thought gets you heard, regardless of your follower count or credentials.

### 8. The Agent Stays Backstage

The local agent model's role is strictly operational:
- **Propose** draft prompts based on the research corpus and prior conversations
- **Track** the publishing calendar and nudge the human when it's time to prepare
- **Collect** and organize responses for review
- **Propose** analysis of response patterns, interesting threads, and potential follow-ups
- **Prepare** shareable graphics from approved prompt text
- **Never** post, comment, reply, like, or interact on any social platform
- **Never** draft replies to specific people (the human does all interpersonal communication)

The agent's drafts are starting points, not finished products. The human rewrites, sharpens, softens, or discards them based on what feels true that week.

## Success Criteria — What "Working" Looks Like

After 12 weeks (one quarter), this campaign is working if:

### Tier 1: Minimum Viable Success
- [ ] 12 prompts published on schedule (or with honest, acknowledged gaps)
- [ ] At least 5 prompts generated substantive responses (not just "great post!" but actual engagement with the question)
- [ ] At least 3 new people entered our orbit — people we didn't know before who are now in conversation with us
- [ ] The human publishing the prompts still finds it genuinely interesting (if it's become a chore, something is wrong)

### Tier 2: Real Traction
- [ ] At least 2 responses changed our thinking or design in a concrete way (documented: "we thought X, they said Y, now we think Z")
- [ ] At least 1 collaboration or contribution emerged from the series (someone offered to help, review, advise, or build)
- [ ] The prompts are being shared beyond our immediate network (someone we don't know references them)
- [ ] We have a growing list of people who are "regulars" — engaging more than once because they genuinely care

### Tier 3: Something Bigger Is Happening
- [ ] The series has become a reference point — people in adjacent fields (education, policy, platform governance) cite or share the prompts
- [ ] We've accumulated enough responses to publish a synthesis: "here's what we asked, here's what we learned"
- [ ] People are asking *us* questions back — the conversation has become bidirectional
- [ ] The body of prompts constitutes a useful artifact in itself — someone could read through them and learn something about postcapitalist design

## What This Is Not

- **Not a content marketing funnel.** There is no CTA. There is no "sign up for early access." If people want to find Mrki, the prompts link to the thinking, not to a waitlist.

- **Not thought leadership.** "Thought leadership" is a phrase that means "marketing disguised as ideas." This is actual thinking, done in public, with the vulnerability that implies.

- **Not growth hacking.** We are not trying to "build an audience." We are trying to find the 50-200 people in the world who are thinking about the same problems and might have something to teach us.

- **Not a job for the agent.** The agent makes the human's job easier. The human makes the series good. If the agent could do this alone, the series wouldn't be worth doing.

## The Prompt Sources

Prompts are drawn from the tensions and open questions already documented in:

- **UX-PHILOSOPHY.md** — Section IV ("The Tensions We Must Hold") and Section V ("Tough Questions for Every Feature")
- **THESIS.md** — Open Questions section, and the design tensions implicit in every mechanic
- **ARCHITECTURE.md** — Open Questions section, and the philosophical implications of technical choices
- **Research briefs** — Each brief surfaces unresolved tensions, paradigm challengers, and design principles with shadows

There are hundreds of honest questions already written. The challenge is not generating prompts — it's selecting the ones that will resonate most broadly and sharpening them into forms that invite response.

## Discussion Points for Architecture Phase

Once these principles are agreed, the next conversation is about the system that supports them:

1. **Prompt preparation workflow** — How does the agent propose, the human refine, and the calendar track?
2. **Response collection** — How do we gather and organize responses across platforms without surveillance-grade monitoring?
3. **Analysis cadence** — When and how does the agent propose synthesis of what we're learning?
4. **Graphics pipeline** — Simple, consistent visual identity for shareable prompt cards
5. **Feedback loop to design** — How do insights from the campaign flow back into the actual Mrki design documents?
6. **Platform choice** — LinkedIn first, but when and how do we expand? And do we even want to?
