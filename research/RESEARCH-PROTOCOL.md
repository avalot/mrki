# Academic Research Protocol: Designing Constructive Digital Networks

## Research Question

**What makes great, constructive, respectful, helpful, self-regulating, non-addictive, digitally-mediated or digitally-facilitated networks?**

Sub-questions:
- What has worked historically? What has failed, and why?
- What are the critical design choices that determine whether a network becomes healthy or toxic?
- What should a platform strategy address, avoid, or safely deprioritize?
- What are the underlying values and viewpoints that must be held in tension?

## Context

We are designing a social platform (Mrki) for teens (13–18) that rejects the attention economy model. We are questioning assumptions that most platforms take as given:

- That content should be instant and free
- That access should be frictionless and unlimited
- That more engagement is always better
- That synchronous/real-time is superior to asynchronous/delayed

We are exploring design primitives that have no precedent in mainstream social media:
- **Postage**: a small "gratitude currency" cost to post publicly (0.1 for text, more for heavy media)
- **Postal pacing**: far-away content delivered daily like mail; local content more frequent
- **Request-based archives**: old content isn't browsable on demand — you request it, it arrives tomorrow
- **Weight-aware posting**: the attention you request from others has a measurable, visible cost
- **Carbon-conscious design**: digital actions have externalities; making them visible changes behavior

## Research Domains

### Domain 1: Attention Economy, Persuasive Technology & Digital Wellbeing

**Fields:** Media studies, HCI, behavioral psychology, public health

**Core questions:**
- What are the specific mechanisms by which social platforms become addictive?
- What interventions have been shown to reduce compulsive use without killing engagement?
- What does "healthy engagement" look like, empirically?
- What is the state of research on teen-specific digital wellbeing?

**Key figures to investigate:**
- Tristan Harris / Center for Humane Technology — "Time Well Spent," humane design
- B.J. Fogg — persuasive technology, behavior design (Stanford)
- Nir Eyal — "Hooked" model (habit-forming design), later "Indistractable" (counter-argument)
- Adam Alter — "Irresistible" (behavioral addiction to technology)
- Jean Twenge — "iGen" (generational research on smartphones and mental health)
- Jonathan Haidt — "The Anxious Generation" (social media and teen mental health)
- Amy Orben — nuanced quantitative research challenging simplistic "screens are bad" narratives
- Andrew Przybylski — Oxford Internet Institute, "Goldilocks hypothesis" of screen time

**Dialectics to explore:**
- Haidt/Twenge (social media is causally harmful) vs. Orben/Przybylski (effect sizes are tiny, moral panic)
- Fogg (design determines behavior) vs. individual agency arguments
- Abstinence models (ban screens) vs. harm reduction (design better screens)

**Methodological tensions:**
- Correlational vs. causal evidence in screen time research
- Self-report vs. behavioral measurement
- Population-level statistics vs. individual vulnerability

### Domain 2: Commons Governance & Self-Regulating Communities

**Fields:** Political economy, institutional economics, cooperative studies, digital governance

**Core questions:**
- What makes self-governing commons sustainable vs. collapsing into tragedy?
- How do successful online communities maintain quality without top-down control?
- What governance structures work at different scales (10, 100, 1000, 100K people)?
- How does governance interact with economic incentives?

**Key figures to investigate:**
- Elinor Ostrom — Nobel laureate, "Governing the Commons," 8 principles for managing shared resources
- Yochai Benkler — "The Wealth of Networks," commons-based peer production
- Clay Shirky — "Here Comes Everybody," collective action and group dynamics online
- Primavera De Filippi — blockchain governance, "Blockchain and the Law"
- Nathan Schneider — "Everything for Everyone," platform cooperativism
- Dmytri Kleiner — "Telekommunist Manifesto," venture communism, peer production critique
- Jo Freeman — "The Tyranny of Structurelessness" (critical: what happens when you claim "no hierarchy")

**Dialectics to explore:**
- Ostrom's polycentric governance vs. centralized platform control
- Freeman's warning (structurelessness creates invisible hierarchies) vs. flat organization ideals
- Commons-based peer production (Benkler) vs. critique that it depends on unpaid labor (Kleiner)
- Governance by code ("code is law" — Lessig) vs. governance by community norms

**Historical cases:**
- Wikipedia's governance evolution (barnstars, admins, arbitration committee, bureaucratic creep)
- Reddit's moderation crisis cycle (volunteer mods, burnout, policy conflicts)
- Slashdot's karma and metamoderation system (early reputation mechanics)
- Stack Overflow's reputation system (what worked, what created toxic dynamics)
- Early WELL, Usenet, BBS governance models

### Domain 3: Friction, Pace & the Design of Slowness

**Fields:** Interaction design, philosophy of technology, slow movement, media ecology

**Core questions:**
- When is friction a feature rather than a bug?
- What historical communication systems used pace/cost as design tools (postal systems, telegrams)?
- What does the "slow technology" movement propose, and what evidence supports it?
- How does asynchronous communication change the quality of discourse?

**Key figures to investigate:**
- Lars Hallnäs & Johan Redström — "Slow Technology: Designing for Reflection" (seminal paper)
- Carl Honoré — "In Praise of Slowness," slow movement philosophy
- Marshall McLuhan — "The medium is the message," media ecology
- Neil Postman — "Amusing Ourselves to Death," critique of TV/speed culture; "Technopoly"
- Ivan Illich — "Tools for Conviviality," convivial vs. manipulative tools
- Hartmut Rosa — "Social Acceleration" and "Resonance," sociology of speed
- Jenny Odell — "How to Do Nothing: Resisting the Attention Economy"

**Dialectics to explore:**
- Friction as care vs. friction as exclusion (who gets slowed down? who can afford stamps?)
- Postal metaphor (beautiful pacing) vs. real postal systems (class-based access, censorship)
- Slow tech idealism vs. teens' actual desire for fast communication with friends
- Asynchronous depth vs. synchronous spontaneity — both have value

**Historical precedents:**
- Letter-writing culture and what it produced (quality of 18th/19th century correspondence)
- Telegram pricing and how cost-per-word shaped communication style
- Early email culture (pre-smartphone) — asynchronous, thoughtful, newsletter-like
- Zine culture — slow, deliberate, handmade media distribution
- Are.na — a "slow social network" for collecting and connecting ideas

### Domain 4: Adolescent Development, Identity & Social Needs

**Fields:** Developmental psychology, education, sociology of youth

**Core questions:**
- What do adolescents actually need from social environments (not what adults think they need)?
- How does identity formation work in digital spaces? Is pseudonymity helpful or harmful?
- What role does risk-taking play in healthy development, and how should platforms handle it?
- What does "agency" mean for a 14-year-old, and how is it different from an adult's agency?

**Key figures to investigate:**
- Danah Boyd — "It's Complicated: The Social Lives of Networked Teens" (ethnographic, empathetic)
- Sonia Livingstone — LSE, children's rights in digital environments, EU Kids Online
- Mizuko Ito — "Hanging Out, Messing Around, and Geeking Out," connected learning
- Erik Erikson — identity formation stages (foundational developmental psychology)
- Sherry Turkle — "Alone Together," "Reclaiming Conversation" (technology and relationships)
- Howard Gardner & Katie Davis — "The App Generation" (how apps shape identity/creativity)

**Dialectics to explore:**
- Protection vs. agency (keeping teens safe vs. letting them take risks and grow)
- Boyd (teens are sophisticated navigators of digital spaces) vs. Haidt (teens are vulnerable to platform design)
- Turkle (technology diminishes authentic connection) vs. Ito (technology enables new forms of genuine connection)
- Adult projection (what adults fear about teen digital life) vs. teen reality (what teens actually experience)

**Critical tensions for Mrki:**
- We want teen governance, but adolescent brains are still developing executive function
- We want pseudonymity, but it can enable bullying as easily as it enables exploration
- We want to slow things down, but teens' social lives move fast and FOMO is real
- We want to reward depth, but some teens express themselves through rapid, playful, ephemeral exchange

### Domain 5: Economics of Attention, Digital Externalities & Alternative Currencies

**Fields:** Ecological economics, information economics, mechanism design, behavioral economics

**Core questions:**
- Can the externalities of digital communication (attention cost, carbon, cognitive load) be priced without creating inequality?
- What do we know about micropayment/microcost systems and human behavior?
- How do "postage" or "stamp" systems change communication patterns?
- What is the actual carbon footprint of digital media, and is making it visible effective?

**Key figures to investigate:**
- Herbert Simon — "a wealth of information creates a poverty of attention" (foundational)
- Tim Wu — "The Attention Merchants," history of attention as commodity
- Jaron Lanier — "Who Owns the Future?," micropayments, data dignity
- E.F. Schumacher — "Small is Beautiful," economics as if people mattered
- Kate Raworth — "Doughnut Economics," ecological boundaries for economic activity
- Cory Doctorow — "enshittification" thesis, platform decay dynamics
- Vitalik Buterin — mechanism design for digital commons (relevant even without blockchain)

**Dialectics to explore:**
- Micropayments (Lanier: pay for everything) vs. commons (Benkler: share freely)
- Making externalities visible (carbon labels on posts) vs. creating anxiety/guilt
- Postage as equalizer (everyone pays the same) vs. postage as barrier (poor kids post less)
- Attention as scarce resource to be protected vs. attention as gift to be given freely

**Critical tensions for Mrki:**
- A "gratitude stamp" is a beautiful metaphor — but does it punish the kid who communicates most?
- Weight-based posting costs mirror real externalities — but may silence verbose, expressive, or neurodiverse users
- Carbon-conscious design is admirable — but the actual carbon footprint of a text post is negligible; is the metaphor dishonest?
- Free DMs but paid public posts creates a public/private split — does this discourage public contribution?

### Domain 6: Network Science, Dunbar Numbers & Social Graph Health

**Fields:** Network science, evolutionary anthropology, computational social science

**Core questions:**
- What are the natural limits of human social networks, and how should platforms respect them?
- What network topologies produce healthy vs. toxic dynamics?
- How does information spread differently in small-world vs. scale-free networks?
- What is the relationship between network structure and community quality?

**Key figures to investigate:**
- Robin Dunbar — Dunbar's number (150), social brain hypothesis, layers of social intimacy
- Albert-László Barabási — scale-free networks, preferential attachment
- Mark Granovetter — "The Strength of Weak Ties" (foundational sociology of networks)
- Nicholas Christakis & James Fowler — "Connected," social contagion in networks
- Zeynep Tufekci — "Twitter and Tear Gas," networked social movements
- Eli Pariser — "The Filter Bubble," algorithmic curation and polarization

**Dialectics to explore:**
- Dunbar limits (humans can't maintain more than ~150 meaningful relationships) vs. platform incentives to maximize connections
- Weak ties (Granovetter: crucial for opportunity and diverse information) vs. strong ties (deep relationships that provide support)
- Network effects as growth engine vs. network effects as quality degrader (Eternal September)
- Algorithmic curation (necessary at scale) vs. chronological/human curation (authentic but noisy)

### Domain 7: Historical Precedents — What Worked, What Died, What Degraded

**Fields:** Internet history, media history, cultural studies

**Core questions:**
- What were the qualities of pre-enshittification social networks that made them "great"?
- Why did they degrade, and was degradation inevitable or a choice?
- What alternative/underground digital communities have maintained quality over decades?
- What can we learn from non-digital community design (cohousing, kibbutzim, clubs, guilds)?

**Cases to investigate:**
- **Early Facebook (2004–2009)** — college network, real identity, chronological feed, no ads initially. What changed and why?
- **LiveJournal** — long-form, community-driven, interest-based. Survived years. Killed by ownership change.
- **Flickr (pre-Yahoo)** — genuine creative community, respectful norms, "interestingness" algorithm
- **MetaFilter** — $5 entry fee as quality filter. 25+ years of sustained community quality. The canonical "friction works" example.
- **The WELL** — one of the first online communities (1985). "You Own Your Own Words." Still exists.
- **Scuttlebutt (SSB)** — P2P, offline-first social network. Technically similar to Mrki's architecture. Small but devoted community.
- **Are.na** — paid, slow, no algorithm, creative/intellectual community. Niche but thriving.
- **Ravelry** — crafting community. Millions of users. Self-moderated. Niche passion + good governance = longevity.
- **Craigslist** — deliberately non-optimized, minimal design, resisted enshittification for 25+ years
- **Cohousing / intentional communities** — physical-world governance models that parallel digital communities
- **Kibbutzim** — rotating responsibility, communal governance, evolution over decades
- **Guild systems** — reputation-based, apprenticeship model, self-regulating professional communities

## Research Method per Domain

For each domain, the research agent should:

1. **Survey the field**: who are the key thinkers, what are the major schools of thought, what's the current consensus (if any)?
2. **Surface the dialectics**: what are the live disagreements? Where do luminaries contradict each other? What's unresolved?
3. **Identify paradigm-challengers**: who is saying something unfashionable, radical, or counterintuitive that might be important?
4. **Extract design principles**: from the research, what concrete principles should guide Mrki's UX design?
5. **Flag critical tensions**: where do principles conflict? Where must Mrki make a choice that some research would support and other research would oppose?
6. **Name the blindspots**: what does this field tend to ignore, undervalue, or assume away?

## Output Format

Each agent should produce a research brief (under 1000 words) structured as:

- **Field overview** (2-3 sentences)
- **Key thinkers and positions** (the map of the intellectual landscape)
- **What works** (evidence-based findings relevant to Mrki)
- **What fails** (cautionary findings)
- **Live dialectics** (unresolved tensions the field is debating)
- **Paradigm challengers** (radical/unfashionable positions worth considering)
- **Design principles for Mrki** (concrete, actionable)
- **Critical tensions** (where principles conflict — decisions we must make, not avoid)
- **Blindspots** (what this field misses that other fields cover)

## How This Feeds Into Design

The research briefs will be synthesized into a **UX Philosophy** document that:
1. States the values and tensions explicitly
2. Proposes design principles with evidence
3. Identifies where we must make judgment calls (not just "follow the research")
4. Challenges our own assumptions (postage, pacing, stamps — are they good ideas or romantic projections?)
