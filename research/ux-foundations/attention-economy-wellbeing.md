# Domain 1: Attention Economy, Persuasive Technology & Digital Wellbeing

## Field Overview

The attention economy describes a market where human attention is the scarce resource that platforms compete to capture and monetize. Persuasive technology design (Fogg, Stanford) provides the toolkit; behavioral addiction research (Alter, Eyal) describes the outcome; and digital wellbeing research (Orben, Przybylski, Twenge, Haidt) measures the damage — though the field is deeply divided on how much damage there actually is.

## Key Thinkers and Positions

**The "social media is causally harmful" camp:**
- **Jonathan Haidt** ("The Anxious Generation," 2024) — argues phone-based childhood is the primary driver of the teen mental health crisis. Advocates for smartphone bans in schools, no social media before 16, and phone-free childhood norms. Cites correlational evidence at population scale.
- **Jean Twenge** ("iGen," 2017; "Generations," 2023) — identified the post-2012 inflection point in teen depression correlating with smartphone adoption. Critiqued for ecological fallacy (population trends don't prove individual causation).

**The "effect sizes are tiny, it's moral panic" camp:**
- **Amy Orben** (Cambridge) — rigorous large-N studies finding screen time effects on wellbeing are comparable to wearing glasses or eating potatoes. Her 2019 *Nature Human Behaviour* paper analyzed 350,000+ participants; technology use explained 0.4% of variation in wellbeing.
- **Andrew Przybylski** (Oxford Internet Institute) — "Goldilocks hypothesis": moderate digital engagement is associated with *better* wellbeing than zero engagement. Critiques methodological weakness in alarmist research. Has pushed for preregistered studies and open data.

**The "design is destiny" camp:**
- **B.J. Fogg** (Stanford Persuasive Technology Lab) — Fogg Behavior Model (motivation × ability × trigger). Trained the founders of Instagram and other engagement-maximizing platforms. His framework explains *how* platforms hook users: reduce friction (ability), use variable rewards (motivation), and deploy push notifications (triggers).
- **Tristan Harris** (Center for Humane Technology) — former Google design ethicist. "Time Well Spent" movement. Argues the problem is not screens per se but the business model: advertising requires maximizing engagement, which requires exploiting psychological vulnerabilities.
- **Nir Eyal** ("Hooked," 2014; "Indistractable," 2019) — described the Hook Model (trigger → action → variable reward → investment cycle), then pivoted to arguing users should build personal responsibility. The tension in his work mirrors the field's debate: is it the platform's fault or the user's?

**The nuanced middle:**
- **Adam Alter** ("Irresistible," 2017) — describes behavioral addiction mechanics (variable intermittent reinforcement, absence of stopping cues, social approval) without taking a maximalist position on harm.
- **Surgeon General Vivek Murthy** (2023 advisory) — declared social media a "profound risk" to youth mental health, signaling institutional concern even as the evidence remains debated.

## What Works (Evidence-Based)

1. **Removing variable reward schedules reduces compulsive checking.** The like-count/notification pattern is the core addiction mechanic. Hiding like counts (Instagram's experiment) reduced social comparison anxiety. Mrki's "Thank" mechanic (private, non-aggregated) directly addresses this.

2. **Friction interventions improve deliberation.** A 2024 meta-analysis of 94 studies found that adding planning friction (delays, confirmations, reflection prompts) improved decision quality. Mrki's pre-post reflection nudges are supported.

3. **Notification batching reduces compulsive checking.** Studies show moving from push notifications to batched/digest delivery reduces app-opens-per-day without reducing satisfaction. Mrki's daily digest model is supported.

4. **Content recall improves with slower consumption.** Research on design friction (scrolling delays, content limits) found users remember more and report higher satisfaction when consumption is paced rather than infinite.

5. **Prosocial features shift platform culture.** Platforms that foreground gratitude, helping, and collaboration (vs. performance and competition) produce measurably different interaction patterns.

6. **Australia's under-16 ban (2025-2026)** is producing early data: teens report reduced anxiety but also social isolation and FOMO. Supports the "better alternative" thesis — bans create demand, but demand needs somewhere to go.

## What Fails

1. **Pure screen-time limits don't work.** Meta-analyses consistently find that *what* teens do online matters more than *how long*. Time-based restrictions miss the point.

2. **Parental monitoring backfires.** Surveillance-based approaches (Bark, Qustodio) damage trust and push teens toward hidden behaviors. This supports Mrki's anti-surveillance stance.

3. **Self-regulation advice is insufficient.** Eyal's "Indistractable" approach (build personal habits) ignores that platforms are specifically designed to defeat self-regulation. Design must change, not just users.

4. **Heavy-handed moderation kills communities.** Over-moderation produces sterile spaces that teens abandon. Under-moderation produces toxicity. The sweet spot is community-driven moderation with graduated consequences — exactly Mrki's approach.

## Live Dialectics

**Haidt vs. Orben:** Is social media causally harmful to teens, or is it a moral panic with tiny effect sizes? The honest answer: probably both. Population-level effects are small; vulnerable subpopulations (girls, pre-existing anxiety, heavy users) show larger effects. Mrki should design for the vulnerable, not the median.

**Design determinism vs. agency:** Does platform design control behavior, or do users have meaningful choice? Fogg's framework says design is powerful but not absolute. Mrki's approach (design for good defaults, allow user autonomy) threads this needle.

**Abstinence vs. harm reduction:** Should teens be kept off social media entirely, or given better social media? The French law attempts abstinence; Mrki is the harm reduction play. Evidence from substance abuse policy strongly favors harm reduction when the behavior is widespread.

## Paradigm Challengers

- **Orben's "Sisyphus" argument** (2025): every generation has a moral panic about new media (novels, radio, TV, video games, smartphones). The *pattern* of panic should make us skeptical of any single iteration. This doesn't mean there's no problem — it means the problem is likely more nuanced than "phones bad."

- **"Addictive motivational scaffolds" framework** (Synthese, 2025): argues the problem isn't attention capture but that platforms provide *substitute* motivation structures that crowd out intrinsic motivation development. Teens don't learn to be self-directed because the platform directs them. This is a deeper critique than "addiction" and directly relevant to Mrki's design: we should support intrinsic motivation, not replace it with platform-designed motivation.

- **Przybylski's methodological critique**: most screen-time research uses self-report data (unreliable), non-preregistered hypotheses (fishing for significance), and cross-sectional designs (can't prove causation). The field's evidence base is weaker than its policy influence suggests.

## Design Principles for Mrki

1. **Eliminate variable intermittent reinforcement.** No like counts, no follower counts, no algorithmic feed. Thanks are private and non-aggregated. This removes the core addiction mechanic.
2. **Build in stopping cues.** Daily digests have a natural end. Finite feeds say "that's everything for now." The platform does not try to keep you scrolling.
3. **Pace, don't block.** Friction works better than restriction. Delay before publish, digest delivery, weight-aware posting — these slow without forbidding.
4. **Design for the vulnerable.** If 5% of teens are highly susceptible to compulsive use, design the platform for them. Features that help the vulnerable won't harm the resilient.
5. **Support intrinsic motivation.** Projects, collaboration, creative tools, governance participation — give teens reasons to engage that come from *their* goals, not the platform's engagement metrics.
6. **Batch notifications.** No push notifications for non-urgent content. Digest model for posts. Real-time only for DMs and time-sensitive event coordination.

## Critical Tensions

- **We want slow and deliberate, but the evidence says moderate engagement is fine.** Przybylski's Goldilocks data suggests we might be over-correcting. Some spontaneous, playful, fast interaction is healthy.
- **We remove vanity metrics, but some feedback is necessary for learning.** Teens need to know their contributions are valued. Thanks are private — is that enough signal? Or does it feel like shouting into a void?
- **We reject surveillance, but we compute invisible reputation.** This is philosophically close to what we criticize in ad-tech platforms (tracking behavior to compute scores). The difference is purpose and transparency of mechanism — but we should be honest about the tension.

## Blindspots

- **Almost all research is WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic).** We know very little about digital wellbeing in non-Western contexts where Mrki might expand.
- **Neurodiversity is underexamined.** ADHD, autism, and other conditions interact with platform design in ways the literature barely addresses. Friction-heavy design may be hostile to neurodiverse users.
- **The field focuses on harm, not flourishing.** We know a lot about what makes platforms harmful. We know almost nothing about what makes them *good* for human development. Mrki is trying to build the positive case with almost no evidence base to guide it.
