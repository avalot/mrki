# Research Protocol: Developmental Progression & Teen Agency

## Framing

We reject the premise that teens are "not ready" for responsibility. We believe that given the right scaffolding, teens can organize, govern, create, and lead — and that doing so is how they develop the capacities adults claim they lack.

We are designing Mrki not as a social media platform but as a **developmental environment** — an MMORPG of real life, where capabilities accrete, character is built through action, communities form around shared endeavor, and coming-of-age is something you *do*, not something that happens to you on your 18th birthday.

This is teen LinkedIn (a corpus of real work), life-Minecraft (building things that matter), and also sports teams, parties, study groups, fan clubs, and support gangs — all in one place, with progressive levels of interconnection and responsibility.

Furthermore: a system designed to support low-executive-function individuals in having real-world impact can serve not only teens with developing EF, but also adults with ADHD, acquired brain injuries, and other conditions. Universal design, not accommodation.

## Research Domains

### Domain A: Coming of Age Across Cultures & History
What rituals, structures, and experiences have human societies used to mark and facilitate the transition from childhood to adulthood? What do these reveal about what teens actually need? How have different cultures treated teen competence and agency?

### Domain B: Game Design as Developmental Architecture
What can MMORPGs, progression systems, and game design teach us about scaffolding capability growth? How do games handle leveling, skill trees, guild governance, and earned responsibility? What's the difference between gamification (extrinsic reward bolted on) and genuine progression (intrinsic growth made visible)?

### Domain C: Scaffolded Autonomy & Executive Function
What does developmental science say about supporting (not replacing) executive function in teens? How do you design for people whose planning, impulse control, and working memory are still developing — without patronizing them? What works for ADHD across age groups?

### Domain D: Youth Organizations That Actually Empower
What real-world organizations have successfully given young people genuine power, responsibility, and agency? What structures made this work? What failed? From scouts to student government to youth parliaments to apprenticeships to revolutionary movements.

### Domain E: Portfolio, Mastery & the Corpus of Work
How do people build a body of work over time? What systems support progressive skill development and visible accomplishment? From craft guilds to academic portfolios to open-source contribution graphs. How does this apply to teens building something they can carry into adulthood?

### Domain F: Peer Support, Mutual Aid & Community Resilience
How do peer support networks form and sustain themselves? What makes some groups resilient and others fragile? How do marginalized communities build mutual aid structures? What can teen-designed support systems learn from these?

### Domain G: Universal Design for Cognitive Diversity
How do we design systems that work for varied executive function levels without creating separate "accommodated" tracks? What does ADHD-friendly design look like? What about autism-friendly design? How do these intersect with teen developmental needs?
