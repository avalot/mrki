# Domain F: Peer Support, Mutual Aid & Community Resilience

## Field Overview

Peer support, mutual aid, and community resilience are distinct but overlapping traditions. Mutual aid is the oldest -- Kropotkin documented it in 1902 as a biological and social fact, not an ideology. Peer support formalized in 20th-century mental health settings, from AA (1935) to Crisis Text Line (2013). Community resilience emerged as a research framework after Hurricane Katrina, consolidated by Norris et al. (2008) into four adaptive capacities: economic development, social capital, information/communication, and community competence. All three traditions share a core insight: people who face similar conditions are often better positioned to help each other than professionals or institutions. The research question for Mrki is whether this holds for teenagers on a digital platform -- and under what structural conditions.

## Key Traditions and What They Teach

**Mutual aid (Kropotkin through Dean Spade).** Kropotkin argued cooperation is more natural and common than competition -- a claim contemporary evolutionary biology broadly supports (Gould, Nowak). The Black Panther Party's survival programs (free breakfast, health clinics, schools) demonstrated mutual aid as political infrastructure: meeting immediate needs while building collective power. ACT UP showed how crisis-driven mutual aid can produce both care and systemic change. Dean Spade's 2020 handbook codified principles for COVID-era networks: horizontal structure, no means-testing, solidarity not charity. COVID mutual aid research found that groups sustained themselves through fluid leadership rotation, pre-existing neighborhood ties, digital communication tools, and explicit values of reciprocity. Groups that burned out typically had fixed leadership, resource exhaustion without external support, or mission drift toward charity models (PMC8563598).

**AA and 12-step programs.** The most enduring peer support system in history, and structurally instructive regardless of one's view of the content. Key structural elements: sponsorship (one-to-one mentoring with a more experienced member), anonymity (reducing status competition), progressive steps (structured path, not open-ended wallowing), service commitment (helping others as part of your own recovery), and the meeting format itself (ritualized sharing with minimal cross-talk). A 2020 Cochrane review found AA/12-step facilitation as effective as other clinical treatments for alcohol use disorder. Sponsorship specifically predicts sustained abstinence, and the quality of the sponsor-sponsee alliance matters more than mere assignment (Recovery Research Institute). Valid critiques: dogmatism around the disease model and higher-power requirement; power dynamics in sponsorship (especially gender-based abuse); limited effectiveness data for populations beyond white American men; and the abstinence-only framing that excludes harm-reduction approaches.

**Restorative justice and circles.** Circle processes -- drawn from Indigenous traditions and formalized by Howard Zehr and Kay Pranis -- create structured space for storytelling, accountability, and repair. In K-12 schools, restorative practices show consistent reductions in suspensions for violent behavior and improvements in school climate (Darling-Hammond & Fronius). Circles work by creating bounded equality: everyone speaks in turn, a talking piece enforces listening, and facilitators hold structure without controlling content. Limitations: effects on academic outcomes and bullying are more mixed; implementation quality varies enormously; and circles can become performative without genuine buy-in from participants holding power.

**Online support communities.** Research on platforms like 7 Cups, Reddit mental health communities, and TrevorProject reveals a consistent pattern: moderated communities produce greater participation, increased civility, and improved emotional outcomes (JMIR 2026). Unmoderated or weakly moderated spaces enable toxic echo chambers -- studies of eating disorder communities found that platforms with weak moderation amplify pro-anorexia rhetoric through network effects (EPJ Data Science, 2025). Effective moderation co-produces policies with the community, uses graduated responses rather than blunt removal, accounts for group dynamics (not just individual posts), and provides clear escalation pathways. Moderators themselves face burnout and must have support structures, boundaries, and relief.

## What Works (Evidence-Based Peer Support Structures)

**Structured roles, not just goodwill.** AA's sponsorship, Crisis Text Line's trained counselors, and school-based peer mentoring all formalize the helping relationship. Research consistently shows that unstructured "just be there for each other" approaches amplify pre-existing symptoms through co-rumination -- especially in adolescent close friendships where friends share attitudes and reinforce each other's distress (PMC6881514). Structure means: defined roles, training, boundaries on scope, and clear handoff to professionals when needed.

**The helper benefits from helping.** The "helper therapy principle" (Riessman, 1965) is one of the most robust findings: providing support improves the helper's own outcomes. AA's service commitment, peer counselor programs, and mutual aid volunteering all show this effect. COVID mutual aid participation increased well-being across emotional experience, social relationships, and sense of control (Mak & Lim, 2021). Mrki implication: the platform should make it easy and valued to give support, not just receive it.

**Training and supervision are non-negotiable.** Peer supporters without training and supervision develop compassion fatigue, make boundary violations, and give harmful advice. Research on peer support workers in mental health found that adequate supervision was viewed as "integral to maintaining emotional wellbeing" (PMC10632624). Programs like Teen Line require 60+ hours of training before teens answer calls. The minimum viable structure includes: training on active listening, clear scope limits (what you can and cannot do), regular check-ins with trained adults, and explicit permission to step back.

**Dense, cohesive networks protect.** Adolescent friendship research shows that dense friendship networks with many interconnections buffer against self-injurious thoughts and behaviors better than a single close friendship (Springer, 2022). Close friendship strength in mid-adolescence predicts higher self-worth and lower anxiety into early adulthood (PMC5821600). Mrki implication: design for overlapping small groups, not isolated dyads.

**Identity-specific spaces matter.** LGBTQ+ youth are significantly more likely to participate in identity communities online (82% out about sexual orientation online vs. 53% in person). GSAs and community-based organizations show measurable effects on self-esteem and reduced substance use (PMC8091042). Online identity spaces serve as essential resources when physical environments are not affirming. The key design tension: maintaining boundaries that create safety without creating echo chambers that prevent growth.

## What Fails (What Makes Peer Support Harmful)

**Co-rumination.** When adolescent friends discuss problems extensively without problem-solving, they amplify each other's distress. Research on "intensity effects" in close friendships shows that high-frequency emotional sharing without structure reinforces depressive symptoms (PMC6881514). Peer support without scaffolding toward action or coping becomes a suffering amplifier.

**Unqualified crisis response.** Teens who encounter suicidal peers without training face vicarious trauma and may give dangerous advice (minimizing risk, promising secrecy, attempting to be the sole lifeline). The research on peer support in mental health consistently emphasizes that peer supporters must have clear escalation pathways and explicit permission to involve professionals.

**Helper exploitation and burnout.** Asking vulnerable people to support other vulnerable people without institutional backing produces burnout. COVID mutual aid groups with fixed leadership burned out fastest. Peer supporters in homelessness services report compassion fatigue indistinguishable from professional burnout (PMC11001661). Without rotation, relief, and recognition, helper roles become traps.

**Behavioral contagion.** Online support communities can normalize and spread harmful behaviors -- self-harm, disordered eating, substance use. This is not hypothetical; it is documented across platforms. The mechanism is social learning in the absence of counter-narratives. Effective moderation must disrupt harmful network dynamics, not just remove individual posts.

**Power dynamics in peer relationships.** Sponsorship in AA, mentoring in schools, and moderation in online communities all create asymmetric relationships. Without accountability structures, these become vectors for manipulation, boundary violation, and abuse. Research on adolescent friendship leaders shows that the most influential teens in friend groups are not always the most destructive -- but influence without accountability is reliably harmful.

## Design Principles for Mrki

1. **Structure the support, not just the space.** Provide frameworks for peer interaction: guided check-ins, structured circles, topic-bounded groups. Open-ended emotional dumping produces co-rumination. Structured sharing with coping orientation produces support.

2. **Train before you deploy.** Any formalized peer support role (moderator, mentor, circle holder) requires training and ongoing supervision. This does not mean professional gatekeeping -- it means skilled community members with access to professional backup.

3. **Build for rotation, not heroism.** Mutual aid sustains through fluid leadership and shared responsibility. Design peer support roles with term limits, handoff protocols, and explicit permission to step back. The platform should make it structurally impossible for one person to become the sole support pillar.

4. **Escalation pathways that don't feel like betrayal.** Teens will not use crisis referrals that feel like snitching. Design warm handoffs: peer supporter stays involved while professional resource enters. Make professional support feel like an extension of community care, not its replacement.

5. **Dense networks over dyadic isolation.** Encourage overlapping small groups rather than single-thread relationships. A teen in crisis should have multiple connection points, not one best friend carrying the load.

6. **Identity spaces with permeable boundaries.** Support affinity groups (LGBTQ+, disability, racial/ethnic) with genuine autonomy over their space -- but design for cross-pollination. Closed groups that never interact with broader community become echo chambers; fully open groups lose the safety that marginalized members need.

7. **The helper therapy principle as core mechanic.** Make giving support as rewarding and visible as receiving it. Not through gamification (badges trivialize care), but through genuine recognition: reciprocal gratitude, community memory of contributions, and visible impact.

8. **Moderation as community function, not policing.** Co-produce moderation norms with the community. Use graduated responses. Support moderators with training, boundaries, and relief. Moderation should feel like tending a garden, not enforcing a code.

## Critical Tensions

**Support without surveillance.** Effective peer support requires knowing when someone is struggling. But monitoring emotional states creates surveillance infrastructure. Tension: the same data that enables a community to catch a falling member enables a platform to track, profile, and commodify distress. Mrki's answer must be structural: support signals should be visible to the community (opt-in), not extracted by the platform.

**Care without codependence.** Mutual aid research shows that reciprocity sustains groups. But adolescent relationships are prone to enmeshment -- "I can't be okay unless you're okay." Design must normalize boundaries, make stepping back a valued act (not abandonment), and prevent any single relationship from becoming a sole lifeline.

**Safety without professional gatekeeping.** The mental health system pathologizes normal adolescent distress and is inaccessible to many teens (cost, stigma, parental consent). But untrained peer support for genuine crises (suicidality, abuse, psychosis) is dangerous. The resolution is layered: most peer support handles normal distress well; trained peer supporters handle moderate crises; professional backup handles acute risk. The layers must be seamless, not siloed.

**Authenticity without contagion.** Honest sharing about mental health struggles is valuable. But research shows behavioral contagion in online communities -- particularly around self-harm and eating disorders. The design challenge: allow authentic expression while structurally disrupting the network dynamics that amplify harmful behavior. This means moderation that is contextual (distinguishing between seeking help and promoting behavior), not keyword-based censorship.

**Solidarity without obligation.** Mutual aid is voluntary. But in close-knit communities, social pressure creates obligation. Teens who cannot sustain a support role should be able to withdraw without shame. This requires that the platform distribute support broadly enough that no individual's withdrawal creates a crisis.

## Blindspots

**Class and access.** Mutual aid research and peer support literature skew toward communities with baseline resources (time, devices, connectivity, emotional bandwidth). Teens in deep poverty, unstable housing, or caregiving roles may not have capacity for peer support even if they need it most. Mrki must account for asymmetric capacity without creating charity dynamics.

**Cultural variation.** Most peer support research comes from Western, English-speaking contexts. Concepts of mutual aid, emotional expression, help-seeking, and community obligation vary dramatically across cultures. What reads as "healthy boundaries" in American individualist culture may read as abandonment in collectivist contexts.

**Neurodivergence.** Peer support models assume neurotypical social capacities: reading emotional cues, reciprocal conversation, sustained attention to others' distress. Neurodivergent teens may need different support structures -- more explicit, more asynchronous, less dependent on real-time emotional attunement.

**The professionalization trap.** As peer support becomes evidence-based and structured, it risks becoming a cheap substitute for professional services rather than a complement. Institutions love peer support because it costs less than professionals. Mrki must resist the pull to make peer support do the work that adults and institutions should be doing.

**Measurement.** Community resilience and mutual aid are notoriously difficult to measure. Norris's four-capacity framework is theoretically robust but hard to operationalize. Mrki will face pressure to demonstrate that its support features "work" -- but the metrics most legible to funders (reduced crisis events, engagement time) may not capture what actually matters (felt sense of belonging, knowing someone has your back).
