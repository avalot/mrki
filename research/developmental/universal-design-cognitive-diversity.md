# Domain G: Universal Design for Cognitive Diversity

## Field Overview

Universal design for cognitive diversity sits at the intersection of disability studies, human-computer interaction, educational psychology, and the neurodiversity movement. The core question -- whether systems designed for neurotypical majorities can be redesigned at the default level to serve cognitive variation -- has moved from a niche accessibility concern to a central design challenge. The field draws on three converging streams: CAST's Universal Design for Learning (UDL) framework, which since the 1990s has argued that flexible, multi-modal design serves all learners better than rigid one-size-fits-all approaches; Sweller's cognitive load theory, which provides the empirical basis for why poorly designed interfaces fail everyone but fail neurodivergent users catastrophically; and the neurodiversity movement's insistence that the problem is environmental, not neurological. The practical question for Mrki is whether a social platform can be built where the ground-floor entrance IS the accessible entrance -- where the default design works across varied executive function levels, sensory profiles, and communication styles without creating separate "accommodated" tracks.

## Key Frameworks and Thinkers

**CAST's UDL Framework (Rose & Meyer, updated to Guidelines 3.0 in 2024):** Three principles -- multiple means of engagement (the "why"), representation (the "what"), and action/expression (the "how"). UDL was developed for education but translates directly to platform design. The key insight is not "offer alternatives for disabled users" but "flexibility at the design level serves the full range of human variation." CAST's 2024 update emphasizes learner agency and self-regulation -- precisely the executive function skills teens are developing.

**John Sweller (Cognitive Load Theory):** Distinguishes intrinsic load (inherent complexity of the task), extraneous load (unnecessary cognitive burden imposed by poor design), and germane load (productive effort that builds understanding). Most social platforms maximize extraneous load through visual clutter, ambiguous navigation, hidden settings, and attention-competing elements. The design goal is to strip extraneous load ruthlessly while preserving and supporting germane load -- the cognitive work that actually matters.

**Damian Milton (Double Empathy Problem, 2012):** The breakthrough reframe: autistic people do not lack empathy; rather, there is a mutual comprehension gap between neurotypes. Communication difficulties are bidirectional. This demolishes the deficit model that drives most "accessibility" approaches and implies that platform design should not "translate" autistic communication into neurotypical norms but should support multiple communication styles as equally valid. Milton's 2024 *Double Empathy Reader* explicitly explores universal design as a tool for bridging this gap.

**Nick Walker (Neurodiversity Paradigm):** Defines neurodiversity as natural cognitive variation -- not pathology requiring correction. Walker distinguishes innate neurodivergence (autism, ADHD, dyslexia) from acquired conditions, arguing the former are identity-constitutive. The design implication: "accommodation" that asks neurodivergent users to perform neurotypicality is assimilation, not inclusion.

**Calm Technology (Amber Case, drawing on Weiser & Brown):** Technology should inform without demanding attention. The principles -- minimal notification, peripheral awareness, user-controlled engagement -- align precisely with what neurodivergent users need but also describe what all users increasingly want as digital fatigue spreads.

## What Works: Evidence-Based Universal Design Principles

**Explicit social norms over implicit ones.** Autistica/Queen Mary University (2023) found that autistic users struggle most on platforms where social rules are unwritten. Platforms that spell out community norms in clear, multi-format guidelines serve autistic users directly -- and reduce conflict, misunderstanding, and moderation burden for everyone. Mrki's visible posting costs and explicit weight system already externalize social norms that are typically invisible.

**Task decomposition and visual progress.** ADHD-focused tools like Goblin Tools (AI-powered task breakdown with adjustable granularity) and Tiimo (visual timeline with color-coded routines and focus timers) demonstrate that breaking complex actions into concrete steps with visible progress dramatically improves task initiation and completion. This is not an ADHD accommodation -- it is good information architecture.

**Sensory control at the user level.** The Autistica research documents that pop-ups, auto-playing videos, and competing visual stimuli are primary barriers for autistic users. "Calm Mode" or "Focus Mode" options that simplify interfaces, dim colors, and remove decorative elements benefit sensory-sensitive users and also reduce cognitive load for everyone. The key: these should be design defaults with the option to add complexity, not complexity defaults with the option to simplify.

**Asynchronous-first communication.** Autistic users report greater calm and control in asynchronous interactions. Users with ADHD benefit from reduced pressure to respond immediately. Teens with developing executive function benefit from time to compose thoughtful responses. Mrki's postal pacing model is already aligned with this principle.

**Predictable, consistent interfaces.** Placing navigation elements in standard locations, maintaining consistent interaction patterns, and avoiding surprise layout changes reduces cognitive load for neurodivergent users and improves usability metrics for all users. Predictability is not boring -- it is cognitively generous.

**Decision fatigue reduction.** ADHD-friendly app design research consistently identifies decision fatigue as a core failure mode. Smart defaults, curated options, guided flows, and AI-assisted prioritization reduce the number of decisions required to accomplish a goal. For Mrki: limiting posting options, providing templates, and making the "right" action the easiest action.

## What Fails: Approaches That Stigmatize or Separate

**"Accessibility mode" as separate track.** Any design that requires users to self-identify as needing accommodation creates stigma. Separate interfaces mean separate maintenance, inevitable feature lag, and the implicit message that the "real" product is for neurotypical users. The architectural analogy: a ramp bolted onto the side of a building versus a building where the main entrance is level.

**Retrofitted accommodations.** Adding screen readers, alt-text, or simplified views after the core product is designed means accessibility is always playing catch-up. The Autistica research documents that sensory needs of autistic users are "rarely considered" in digital design -- not because designers are hostile, but because sensory-friendly design was never part of the original brief.

**Deficit-based framing.** Tools that frame neurodivergent users as broken -- "compensating" for executive function "deficits" -- misunderstand both the neurodiversity paradigm and the practical design challenge. The goal is not to make ADHD users behave like neurotypical users; it is to build environments where different cognitive styles produce effective outcomes.

**Information overload as "choice."** Platforms that offer extensive customization options while burying them in complex settings menus create an illusion of control. The users who most need customization (high sensory sensitivity, low executive function) are least equipped to navigate 47 nested preferences. Meaningful control means a small number of high-impact choices, not exhaustive configuration.

## The Curb Cut Opportunities

These are features designed for neurodivergent users that would make Mrki better for everyone:

**Visible social costs (already in Mrki's design).** Postage and weight systems externalize what is normally invisible cognitive labor -- estimating how much attention you are asking of others. This serves users who struggle with social calibration (common in autism) AND reduces the broadcast-everything dynamic that degrades all social platforms.

**Structured posting templates.** Offering optional templates ("sharing a project," "asking for help," "celebrating something") reduces blank-page paralysis for ADHD users, provides social scripts for autistic users, and improves content quality for all users by channeling expression into purposeful forms.

**Time-aware design.** Visual timers, "you've been here X minutes" indicators, and session boundaries address ADHD time blindness and autistic hyperfocus -- but also help every teen (and adult) manage a resource they chronically underestimate.

**Finite feeds with clear endpoints.** Replacing infinite scroll with bounded content (daily digest, "you've seen everything") provides the stopping cues that Alter identifies as missing from addictive platforms. This serves ADHD users (who lack internal stopping cues) and everyone else (who have been trained out of them).

**Interest-based social architecture.** The Autistica research recommends helping users connect through shared interests rather than social-graph proximity. Interest-based connection serves autistic users (who often prefer topic-focused interaction) and all teens (who are actively exploring identity through interests rather than established social hierarchies).

**Low-friction mood and energy check-ins.** Tiimo's integration of mood tracking alongside task management reflects a principle: knowing your current state helps you make better decisions about engagement. A "how are you feeling?" prompt before posting serves emotional self-regulation across all neurotypes.

## Design Principles for Mrki

1. **Default to calm, opt into intensity.** The baseline interface should be low-stimulation, predictable, and sensory-friendly. Users who want more visual richness, faster pacing, or more social signals can add them. Never the reverse.

2. **Externalize the invisible.** Social costs, time passing, attention requested, community norms -- make visible what other platforms leave to intuition. This serves users with weak social intuition (autism), weak time perception (ADHD), and developing social cognition (all teens).

3. **Scaffold without patronizing.** Offer structure (templates, step-by-step flows, visual guides) as the default path, with the option to bypass for users who do not need it. The scaffold is not the "easy mode" -- it is the entrance.

4. **Support multiple communication modalities.** Text, voice, image, video, structured templates. Not as accessibility accommodations but as co-equal expression channels. Dyslexic users benefit from voice; autistic users may prefer text; all users benefit from choice.

5. **Reduce decisions, not options.** Smart defaults that work for 80% of situations, with clear paths to customize. Every decision point is a potential dropout point for low-EF users.

6. **Make norms explicit.** Community guidelines, posting expectations, interaction norms -- spelled out, not implied. What neurotypical users absorb through social osmosis must be available through direct statement.

7. **Bound everything.** Finite feeds, session boundaries, daily limits, visible progress toward "done." Open-ended, infinite experiences are hostile to executive function at every level.

## Critical Tensions

**Scaffolding versus autonomy.** Too much structure feels controlling; too little fails low-EF users. The resolution is scaffolding that can be gradually removed as users develop capacity -- which requires the system to track and respond to individual growth trajectories, raising complexity and privacy questions.

**Simplicity versus expressiveness.** Stripping extraneous cognitive load risks creating a bland, constrained platform that fails to engage teens who crave novelty and self-expression. The challenge is to be calm without being boring -- to offer rich expression within clear structure.

**Universal design versus personalization.** True universal design means one interface that works for everyone. But cognitive diversity is so broad that some personalization seems inevitable. The tension is between the principle (no separate tracks) and the practical reality (a user with high sensory sensitivity and a user seeking stimulation have contradictory needs). The resolution may be a calm default with user-controlled intensity layers.

**Neurodiversity framing versus clinical reality.** The neurodiversity paradigm is politically and ethically important, but some users experience their ADHD or autism as genuinely disabling and want tools that reduce that disability, not platforms that celebrate it. Mrki must hold space for both: design that does not pathologize difference AND design that provides real functional support.

**Explicit norms versus social fluidity.** Making all social norms explicit serves clarity but may create rigidity. Neurotypical social interaction relies on ambiguity, flexibility, and unspoken renegotiation. Over-specifying norms could create a platform that feels bureaucratic rather than social. The balance point is explicit baseline norms with room for organic community evolution.

## Blindspots

**Co-occurring conditions are underserved in research.** Most studies examine ADHD or autism or dyslexia in isolation. But many teens have overlapping conditions (ADHD + autism is estimated at 30-80% co-occurrence). Design that helps one condition may hinder another -- e.g., visual richness that supports ADHD engagement may overwhelm autistic sensory processing. Intersection-aware design is theorized but poorly empirically grounded.

**Cultural variation in neurodivergence.** Nearly all research on ADHD-friendly and autism-friendly design comes from Western, English-speaking contexts. Neurodivergence presents differently across cultures; what counts as "explicit norms" varies by cultural context. Mrki's international ambitions will encounter this gap.

**Socioeconomic access.** The best ADHD tools (Tiimo, Goblin Tools, Focusmate) are paid apps. Teens who most need executive function scaffolding may be least able to afford it. Building these features into a free platform is a genuine equity opportunity, but the research base skews toward users with resources.

**The "supercritical" user.** Design for the lowest executive function level risks infantilizing high-capacity users. Design for the highest level abandons those who need support. The critical user is the one in between -- the teen having a bad day, the ADHD user who is medicated some days and not others, the autistic user who is fine with text but overwhelmed by video. Designing for state variability, not fixed capacity, is the real challenge and is almost entirely unaddressed in the literature.

**Agency and learned helplessness.** Excessive scaffolding can prevent the development of the very executive function skills teens need to build. If the platform does all the self-regulation work, users may never develop internal regulatory capacity. The developmental psychology literature (Vygotsky's zone of proximal development) suggests scaffolding should be calibrated to be just beyond current capacity -- but implementing this adaptively in a social platform is an unsolved design problem.
